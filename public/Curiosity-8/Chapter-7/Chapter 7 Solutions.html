Chapter 7
Particulate Nature of Matter

Curiosity Book - Exercises

1. Choose the correct option. The primary difference between solids and liquids is that the constituent particles are:
(i) closely packed in solids, while they are stationary in liquids.
(ii) far apart in solids and have fixed position in liquids.
(iii) always moving in solids and have fixed position in liquids.
(iv) closely packed in solids and move past each other in liquids.
Answer: (iv) closely packed in solids and move past each other in liquids.
Explanation: In solids, particles are closely packed and vibrate at fixed positions. In liquids, particles can move past each other, allowing flow.
2. Which of the following statements are true? Correct the false statements.
(i) Melting ice into water is an example of the transformation of a solid into a liquid.
Answer: True
Explanation: This is correct. Melting is the process where solid changes to liquid state.

(ii) Melting process involves a decrease in interparticle attractions during the transformation.
Answer: True
Explanation: Heat energy weakens forces between particles during melting.

(iii) Solids have a fixed shape and a fixed volume.
Answer: True
Explanation: This is correct. Solids maintain both fixed shape and volume.

(iv) The interparticle interactions in solids are very strong, and the interparticle spaces are very small.
Answer: True
Explanation: This is correct. Strong forces keep particles closely packed in solids.

(v) When we heat camphor in one corner of a room, the fragrance reaches all corners of the room.
Answer: True
Explanation: This shows diffusion of camphor vapour particles in air.

(vi) On heating, we are adding energy to the camphor, and the energy is released as a smell.
Answer: False
Correction: On heating, camphor sublimates (changes from solid to gas) and releases vapour particles that we detect as smell.
3. Choose the correct answer with justification. If we could remove all the constituent particles from a chair, what would happen?
(i) Nothing will change.
(ii) The chair will weigh less due to lost particles.
(iii) Nothing of the chair will remain.
Answer: (iii) Nothing of the chair will remain.
Explanation: All matter is made up of particles. If we remove all particles from a chair, no matter would be left to form the chair. Without particles, the chair cannot exist.
4. Why do gases mix easily, while solids do not?
Gases mix easily because gas particles are far apart with weak intermolecular forces and move freely in all directions. They can easily occupy spaces between other gas particles.
Solids do not mix because solid particles are tightly packed with strong intermolecular forces. Particles can only vibrate at fixed positions with no space for other solid particles to move between them.
5. When spilled on the table, milk in a glass tumbler flows and spreads out, but the glass tumbler stays in the same shape. Justify this statement.
Milk flows because liquid particles can move past each other and have no fixed shape. Liquids take the shape of their container due to weaker intermolecular forces.
Glass tumbler maintains its shape because solid particles are rigidly arranged at fixed positions with strong intermolecular forces. Solids have both fixed shape and volume.
6. Represent diagrammatically the changes in the arrangement of particles as ice melts and transforms into water vapour.
Draw three diagrams showing:
ICE: Particles very close together in regular pattern
WATER: Particles close but irregular arrangement
WATER VAPOUR: Particles far apart and scattered
Changes: Ice → Water (particles become less organized), Water → Vapour (particles become free and spread far apart). As temperature increases, particle movement and spacing increases.

9. Why does the water in the ocean taste salty, even though the salt is not visible? Explain.
Ocean water tastes salty because salt dissolves in water at the particle level. When salt dissolves, its particles separate and mix uniformly with water particles. These dissolved salt particles are too small to be seen but spread evenly throughout the water, making every drop taste salty.
The salt particles occupy spaces between water particles through the process of dissolution. Although invisible, the salt is still present in dissolved form.
10. Grains of rice and rice flour take the shape of the container when placed in different jars. Are they solids or liquids? Explain.
Both rice grains and rice flour are solids even though they appear to take the shape of containers.
Why they are solids: Each grain of rice and each particle of flour maintains its own fixed shape and volume. They don't flow like liquids but pour due to gravity acting on individual solid pieces.
Why they seem to take container shape: The spaces between solid particles allow them to settle and fill the container. This is different from liquids where particles actually flow and change positions. Think of marbles in a jar - marbles collectively take the jar's shape, but each marble remains a solid sphere.
