(ii) She took another test tube, 1/4 filled with lime water. She removed the balloon from test tube B in such a manner that the gas inside the balloon did not escape. She attached the balloon to the test tube with lime water and shook it well. What do you think she wants to find out?

3. A farmer was planting wheat crops in his field. He added nitrogen-rich fertiliser to the soil to get a good yield of crops. In the neighbouring field, another farmer was growing bean crops, but she preferred not to add nitrogen fertiliser to get healthy crops. Can you think of the reasons?

Answer: Bean plants have a special relationship with bacteria called Rhizobium that live in their root nodules. These bacteria can convert nitrogen gas from the air into nitrogen compounds that plants can use as nutrients. This process is called nitrogen fixation.

Since bean plants can get their own nitrogen with the help of these bacteria, they don't need additional nitrogen fertilizers. Wheat plants, however, don't have this ability, so they need nitrogen-rich fertilizers for healthy growth.

4. Snehal dug two pits, A and B, in her garden. In pit A, she put fruit and vegetable peels and mixed it with dried leaves. In pit B, she dumped the same kind of waste without mixing it with dried leaves. She covered both the pits with soil and observed after 3 weeks. What is she trying to test?

Answer: Snehal is testing how different composting methods affect the decomposition process.

By mixing fruit and vegetable peels with dried leaves in pit A, she is creating ideal conditions for composting. The dried leaves provide carbon-rich material that helps balance the nitrogen-rich food waste, allowing microorganisms to break down the waste more efficiently.

In pit B, without the dried leaves, the decomposition will be slower and might produce bad odors because the balance between carbon and nitrogen materials is not optimal for the microorganisms.

6. Devise an experiment to test that microorganisms need optimal temperature, air, and moisture for their growth.

Experiment Design:

  1. Take four slices of bread and place each in a separate zip-lock bag.
  2. Label them A, B, C, and D.
  3. Add a few drops of water to slices A, B, and C to create moisture (microorganisms need moisture to grow).
  4. Leave slice D dry (to test the need for moisture).
  5. Keep slice A at room temperature (optimal conditions).
  6. Keep slice B in the refrigerator (low temperature test).
  7. Keep slice C in a warm place like near a heater (high temperature test).
  8. Remove all air from bag B before sealing (to test the need for air).
  9. Observe all slices after 3-5 days for mold growth.

Expected Results:
- Slice A (moist, air, optimal temperature) will show the most mold growth
- Slice B (moist, no air, low temperature) will show little or no growth
- Slice C (moist, air, high temperature) may show some growth but less than A
- Slice D (dry, air, optimal temperature) will show little or no growth

7. Take 2 slices of bread. Place one slice in a plate near the sink. Place the other slice in the refrigerator. Compare after three days. Note your observations. Give reasons for your observations.

Observations:
- The bread slice near the sink will develop mold (fungus growth) faster
- The bread slice in the refrigerator will show little or no mold growth

Reasons:
Microorganisms like mold grow best in warm, moist conditions. The area near the sink provides both moisture and suitable temperature for microorganisms to grow. The refrigerator provides a cold environment that slows down or prevents the growth of most microorganisms.

8. A student observes that when curd is left out for a day, it becomes more sour. What can be two possible explanations for this observation?

Possible Explanations:

  1. The bacteria (Lactobacillus) present in curd continue to ferment the lactose (milk sugar) producing more lactic acid. Increased lactic acid makes the curd taste more sour.
  2. When left out at room temperature, the bacteria become more active and multiply faster, leading to increased fermentation and production of more acid compared to when kept in the refrigerator.
