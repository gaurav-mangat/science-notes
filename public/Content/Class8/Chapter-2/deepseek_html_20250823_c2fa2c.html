Robert Hooke made groundbreaking discoveries using early microscopes:

Observing Onion Cells

Activity 2.2: Studying Plant Cells

Steps to prepare onion cell slides:

  1. Take onion bulb and cut vertically
  2. Remove thin transparent layer (onion peel) with forceps
  3. Stain with safranin for 30 seconds
  4. Rinse with water and mount in glycerin on slide
  5. Cover with coverslip avoiding air bubbles
  6. Observe under microscope

Observation: Rectangular cells closely arranged without spaces

Observing Animal Cells

Activity 2.3: Studying Cheek Cells

Steps to prepare human cheek cell slides:

  1. Gently scrape inside of cheek with toothpick
  2. Spread material on slide with water drop
  3. Add methylene blue stain for contrast
  4. Add glycerin to prevent drying
  5. Cover with coverslip and observe

Observation: Irregular polygon-shaped cells with visible nucleus

Multiple Choice
1. Who is known as the "Father of Microbiology"?
a) Robert Hooke
b) Antonie van Leeuwenhoek
c) Alexander Fleming
d) Louis Pasteur
Answer: b) Antonie van Leeuwenhoek
Explanation: Antonie van Leeuwenhoek is called the Father of Microbiology because he was the first to observe and describe bacteria and other microorganisms using powerful lenses he created.
Multiple Choice
2. Which of these is NOT a part of a basic cell structure?
a) Cell membrane
b) Cytoplasm
c) Nucleus
d) Chlorophyll
Answer: d) Chlorophyll
Explanation: While chlorophyll is present in plant cells (inside chloroplasts), it is not a universal component of all cells. The cell membrane, cytoplasm, and nucleus are fundamental parts of most cells.
Multiple Choice
3. Which microorganism is used in making bread rise?
a) Bacteria
b) Virus
c) Yeast
d) Algae
Answer: c) Yeast
Explanation: Yeast is a fungus that ferments sugars, producing carbon dioxide gas that causes bread dough to rise and become fluffy.
True/False
4. All cells have a cell wall.
Answer: False
Explanation: Only plant cells, fungal cells, and bacterial cells have cell walls. Animal cells do not have cell walls; they only have cell membranes.
Fill in the Blank
5. The control center of the cell that regulates all activities is called the __________.
Answer: nucleus
Explanation: The nucleus contains the cell's genetic material and controls all cellular activities, acting as the command center of the cell.
Short Answer
6. Explain the difference between unicellular and multicellular organisms with examples.
Answer:
Unicellular Organisms: Made of a single cell that performs all life functions.
Examples: Bacteria, Amoeba, Paramecium, Euglena

Multicellular Organisms: Made of many cells that are specialized for different functions.
Examples: Humans, plants, animals, fungi
Short Answer
7. Describe the process of curd formation and the role of microorganisms in it.
Answer: Curd formation involves the fermentation of milk by bacteria:
1. Lactobacillus bacteria are added to warm milk (as starter culture)
2. Bacteria feed on lactose (milk sugar)
3. They produce lactic acid through fermentation
4. The acid causes milk proteins to coagulate, forming curd
5. The process works best at warm temperatures (around 37¬∞C)
Long Answer
8. Explain how microorganisms help in maintaining soil fertility.
Answer:
Microorganisms play crucial roles in maintaining soil fertility through several processes:

1. Decomposition: Bacteria and fungi break down dead organic matter, releasing nutrients back into the soil
2. Nitrogen Fixation: Certain bacteria (like Rhizobium) convert atmospheric nitrogen into forms plants can use
3. Nutrient Cycling: Microbes drive biogeochemical cycles of carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and other elements
4. Soil Structure: Fungal hyphae help bind soil particles together, improving soil texture
5. Disease Suppression: Some microbes protect plants from pathogenic organisms
These processes ensure that soil remains fertile and capable of supporting plant growth.
Application
9. Design an experiment to show that microorganisms are present in the air around us.
Answer:
Experiment to show microorganisms in air:
1. Prepare nutrient agar plates or simply use slices of potato or bread as growth medium
2. Leave some plates exposed to air for different time periods (15 min, 30 min, 1 hour)
3. Keep one plate covered as a control
4. Seal all plates and keep in a warm place for 2-3 days
5. Observe growth of colonies (visible as spots) on exposed plates
6. The control plate should show little or no growth
The colonies that appear represent microorganisms that were present in the air and settled on the plates.
